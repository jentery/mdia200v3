Media + Narrative

Media play significant roles in the construction
of heroes and villains, arcs and perspectives,
plots and subplots, quests and conflicts.

These constructions happen across
formats, including the news.

They are the stuff of fiction and nonfiction.

Narratives can be formulas.

Narratives move stories from equilibrium
to disequilibrium to a new equilibrium
(yes, three acts).

~ a "rags to riches" narrative, for instance ~

Narratives are encoded + decoded and usually
involve themes of transformation.

They can be grand, and they can be minor.

I didn't think of this. Tzvetan Todorov did (1977).

Formulas are entwined
with genres.

From equilibrium to disequilibrium
to a new equilibrium in . . .
science fiction?
horror?
romance?

See Mark Dixon (2020) for more on genre and narrative in Media Studies.

A story is the "whole tale"
of events in chronological
order, from beginning
to end (Parsons).

The Russian Formalists called story the "raw material" or "fabula" organized by a writer.

A plot is the sequence of
events that unfolds in the telling or narrating of
a story (Parsons).

The Russian Formalists called plot "sjuzhet."
Think of books that start at the end or tv shows that
rely heavily on flashing forward and backward in time.

A narrative is a story
and how that story is told, plotted, and arranged.

It "covers both the act of telling or narrating ...
the tale and the tale itself as told" (Parsons).

Think of how people refer to "the narrative" to imply both the story and its narration.

With respect to time in particular,
narratives unfold across a spectrum of linearity.

Linear narrative

The plot proceeds chronologically
(also called a "simple plot").

Nonlinear narrative

The plot does not proceed chronologically
(also called a "complex plot").

Nonlinear devices include analepsis (flashback,
recall), prolepsis (flashforward, foreshadow), and
ellipsis (gap, break).

Let's study Matt Madden's comics for examples.

But what about dynamic story content?
~ like when readers get different endings ~

Emerging Narrative

Audiences contribute to the "whole
tale" of events and how it's told, plotted,
and/or arranged.

The works becomes authoring environments
for audiences to produce their own stories.

What examples come to mind?

Emergence corresponds with the popular desire
to participate in narratives: to become a character
or player in a world, from amusement parks
and escape rooms to video games, theatres of
the imagination, and live action roleplay.

Emergence requires "narrative architecture":
designing spaces that evoke, embed, and enact
stories in the interests of embodied experience.

See Henry Jenkins for more on narrative architecture and environmental storytelling.

What's the power of today's
narrative architectures?

How do they attract + affect us?
